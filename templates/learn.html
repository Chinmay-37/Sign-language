Understanding Sign Language

What is Sign Language?

Sign language is a visual language that uses hand shapes, facial expressions, gestures, and body language to convey meaning. Unlike spoken languages, sign languages are expressed through manual articulation combined with non-manual markers, such as facial expressions. Each sign language has its own grammar and lexicon, making them distinct languages in their own right.

Global Diversity of Sign Languages

There are approximately 300 different sign languages worldwide. These languages are not universal; each country typically has its own native sign language, and they are often not mutually intelligible. For instance, American Sign Language (ASL) is primarily used in the United States, while British Sign Language (BSL) is used in the United Kingdom.

Historical Context

Sign languages have likely existed for centuries, possibly predating spoken languages. They developed naturally within Deaf communities, evolving over time to meet the communication needs of their users.

Notable Developments

In recent years, there has been a growing recognition of the importance of sign languages. For example, Nvidia launched a free AI-powered tool named Signs to teach American Sign Language (ASL), aiming to bridge the gap in learning resources for ASL.

Sign Language in Media

Efforts are being made to make media more accessible to the Deaf community. In the UK, Paramount Pictures started showing captioned films a day before their general release to better serve Deaf and hard-of-hearing audiences.
